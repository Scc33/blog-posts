What are the most important statistics on writing?

Statistics are hard! Let’s break down Medium stats and determine which are the most important

Background

Medium tracks four import statistics: views, reads, read ratio, and fans. Let’s break down each statistic using the analogy of a library. For this analogy you, the writer, are the library and each of your stories is a book in the library.

Definitions

A view is the amount of visitors who clicked on the story page. In our library example, this is a patron who picks up a book, looks at the cover, reads the description on the book jacket, and puts the book back on the shelf.

A read is a count of the visitors who read the entire story. If you were a library, this would be the number of patrons who actually checked out your book to take it home and read it.

Read ratio is a more complicated metric that is calculated from views and reads. Medium defines the read ratio as “an estimate of the percentage of viewers that reached the end of your story.” In other words, it is the number of reads divided by the number of views.

Or if you ran a library it is the number of patrons who checked out a book divided by the number of patrons who picked the book up. If 100 patrons looked at your book in the library, and ten of those checked the book out, then you would have a read ratio of 10%.

The read ratio is the number of reads divided by the number of views.

Finally, the last metric tracked is fans. Fans are the number of unique readers who applauded your story. Whether they clap once or fifty times, each unique reader only counts as one fan. At a library, a fan would be someone who said to a librarian “Hey I enjoyed this book. Can you find more like it?”

Relative Importance

Each statistic on Medium provides some glimpse into how your writings are performing. Views and reads are important aggregate metrics to judge how many people your works are reaching. While reach is important, it can be a poor measure of how readers actually enjoy your content.

Read ratio and fans are measures of engagement. Having a reader engage with your content shows they are enjoying what you write. Unlike the metrics of views and reads that show your reach, the engagements metrics give you an idea of how your content is being enjoyed independent of audience size.

Engagement is the best way to grow an audience.

Whether your audience is one or thousands, keeping engagement high is the best way to make sure you are producing high-quality content.

If we go back to our library example, just picking a book off the shelf isn’t very impactful. Making sure our patrons, our readers, are engaged and likely to check out books is key to our success.
