A take on Apple’s March 2022 Peek Performance Event

Today Apple unveiled a new iPhone, iPad, and M1 chip vastly updating its lineup

Today Apple hosted its first event of 2022. The spring events are typically smaller and more refresh-oriented. However, Apple’s event today contained a few nuggets that show their hand and the direction of the industry.

First, Apple unveiled that “Friday Night Baseball” from the MLB would be coming to Apple TV+. The new feature will allow baseball fans to view two Friday night games during regular season play.

Currently, there is more tv content than ever before. However, sports have remained with the traditional cable tv bundle. Sports seem to be the last great battlefield to come to the streaming wars. Apple is using its massive war chest to break into the sports market. By bringing in baseball, Apple adds another point of differentiation to their products.

Next, Apple updated its cheapest iPhone, the SE, with its most current processor and 5G. The device will cost $429. At this lower price point, Apple engages developing markets and a younger audience who might now otherwise be able to afford iPhones.

Additionally, Apple revealed a more powerful iPad Air featuring many of the features that came to the iPad Pro last year. The new iPad Air brings these powerful features in at a lower price. Similar to the new iPhone SE, this broadens the potential customer base.

Finally, in a surprise announcement, Apple released what looks to be a beast of a processor. The new M1 Ultra is a shocking piece of silicon on paper.

It boasts a whopping 114 billion transistors. All those transistors enable a 20-core CPU, 64-core GPU, 32-core Neural Engine, and 128GB of RAM. Those specs are pretty jaw-dropping.

With this new breed of processor, Apple is entering the very high-end professional market. Apple has marketed pro machines for many years, but many professional applications often remained out of reach. This new chip will enable new workflows inside the Apple ecosystem.

Chips like these are expensive machines and can be revenue generators, but more importantly, they give Apple more control of their ecosystem.

Professionals who previously performed work on other systems might now migrate to powerful Apple hardware. More powerful hardware draws more developers and creators into the ecosystem. More professionals mean more creation which grows the ecosystem. The larger the ecosystem, the greater the lock-in.

So in one swoop, Apple brought sports into their ecosystem, drew in lower-income users with a cheaper iPhone and iPad, and kept professionals engaged. Today feels like another masterclass from Tim Cook and Co.

By Sean Coughlin on March 8, 2022.
