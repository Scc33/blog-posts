Paper money is here to stay for a while

Currency Innovations

Innovation is rapidly changing how we pay for goods and services. In the United States, credit cards were the first innovation that made it easy to pay for goods and services on the go. Next came debit cards and e-commerce. Now Venmo, Cash App, crypto, and neobanks abound are shaking the adage that cash is king.

These tools have made peer-to-peer and large transactions seamless. One of the main motivates for cash is quick transactions. These transactions are customary at small merchants. Yesterday Apple announced a new tap to pay feature coming to iPhones. The new feature will make it easier for merchants to accept new technologies as payment.

Outside the US, progress is even more profound. China’s introduced a digital currency in April 2021. Now the Chinese digital currency is on full display at the 2022 Winter Olympics.

Cash Fights Back

The last few paragraphs described a few innovations that hype the death of cash. However, what has been the effect on the use of physical money?

Despite the widespread disruption narrative in payments, a little-known fact flies under the radar. There is more paper currency in circulation than ever before.

The United States Treasury defines currency in circulation as “paper currency and coins held both by the public and in the vaults of depository institutions.” In December 2021, 2.2 trillion dollars fit that definition. That equates to $8,489 per capita. These numbers spiked during the pandemic and continue to rapidly rise.

Some of these dollars may be flowing out of the US for use worldwide. Additionally, paper money could be flowing to bad actors who value the relative difficulty of tracking cash. Regardless, paper money is still heavily valued and in demand.

New technology is exciting, and new payment technologies have made transactions easier. In the future, further business investment in apps or cryptocurrencies may push out cash. For now, cash is staying in the fight for at least a few more rounds.
